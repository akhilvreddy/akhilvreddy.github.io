What are Xavier and He initializations? Why do they (almost always) help our eval?

January 4, 2025 · 2 min · ML Theory + Code

Deep neural networks suffer from vanishing or exploding activations and gradients, especially as the number of layers increases. This makes it hard for the model to learn, because gradients either:

  1. Become tiny -> layers don’t learn -> training stalls
  2. Become massive -> weights blow up -> unstable training (and your compute gets shot)

This issue happens in both the forward pass and the backward pass.

In the forward pass,

  • If activations shrink layer after layer (because of ReLU/Dense layers with small weights), the signal dies out -> vanishing activations
  • If weights are too large, activations can grow layer by layer -> exploding activations

In the backward pass,

  • Backprop multiplies gradients through the layers via the chain rule
  • If each gradient term is < 1, they shrink exponentially -> vanishing gradients
  • if > 1, they grow -> exploding gradients

On top of this, if you choose tanh as an activation function or have a final prediction head that uses a sigmoid, the flat tails of these functions will only exacerbate this issue (small derivative regions).

If we look at it from a mathematical standpoint:

Let’s say suppose we have layers like:

$$ h^{(l)} = W^{(l)} h^{(l-1)} $$

The variance of activations becomes: $$ Var(h^{(l)}) = Var(W^{(l)}) * Var(h^{(l-1)}) $$

If ( \text{Var}(W) \ne 1 ), this compounding effect leads to vanishing or exploding activations. Hence, our goal is to initialize weights so that: $$ \text{Var}(h^{(l)}) = \text{Var}(h^{(l-1)}) $$
